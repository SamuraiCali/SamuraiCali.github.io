Overview

In addition to on-course sustainability efforts, golf courses are also engaging their communities and raising awareness about environmental issues. Through educational programs, community events, and partnerships with local conservation organizations, golf courses are promoting the importance of environmental stewardship. They are providing opportunities for golfers and community members to learn about sustainable practices and become advocates for the environment.

Governments like Canada have demonstrated a willingness to make hard decisions in the field by regulating the cosmetic usage of chemicals and people working courses would also welcome the change. Superintendents are all genuinely concerned about environmental issues and with a more steady stream of them than ever, change is on the horizon. 

Our Approach

The greener side of golf is a testament to the sport's commitment to environmentalism. With the implementation of eco-friendly management practices and community engagement, golf courses are playing a crucial role in conserving natural resources and protecting biodiversity. As golf continues to evolve, it is heartening to see the industry's dedication to sustainability, ensuring that future generations can enjoy the sport while preserving the planet.

Here are some of the efforts that you can support and participate in.

  • Sustainable Course Design, Many modern golf courses now emphasize sustainable design principles. Courses are designed to preserve natural habitats, integrate native vegetation, and reduce the consumption of water resources. When choosing where to play, opt for courses that follow these practices.
  • Water Conservation, Water usage on golf courses has been a significant concern. To support water conservation efforts, play your part by fixing divots, ball marks, and bunkers to reduce the need for irrigation. Avoid running water when not necessary.
  • Pesticide Reduction, Golf courses are increasingly adopting organic and alternative pest control methods. Encourage your local course to minimize pesticide usage and explore eco-friendly alternatives.
  • Wildlife Preservation, Golf courses can provide valuable habitats for local wildlife. Encourage your course to establish wildlife corridors, birdhouses, and plant native flora. Respect the wildlife you encounter on the course and keep your distance to minimize disturbances.
  • Energy Efficiency: Support golf facilities that are embracing renewable energy sources like solar panels and wind turbines. These energy-efficient practices can help reduce the carbon footprint of golf courses. Remember to be self-aware of your own waste management, be mindful of waste on the course. Dispose of your trash properly and consider carrying a reusable water bottle and bag to minimize waste. Support courses with recycling and waste reduction programs.
  • Get involved in community initiatives that promote sustainable practices, conservation awareness, and eco-friendly golf. Advocate for golf clubs to collaborate with local communities on environmental projects

The Rough

With moderate golf environmentalism being overly reliant on the ‘latest science’ as they negotiate decisions about synthetic chemical use. One must question what are the risks involved in making a radical change to course management in chemically free golf, otherwise known as “organic golf”. In my research through this source, they describe answering the questions surrounding organic golf existence, pros, and cons through the lens of interviewing two key figures in the organic golf space with additional information provided by published interviews and presentations from other organic golf advocates. To answer the question, “Is organic golf good” we must first ask what it is, and in exploring this question a range of views prop up. The most straightforward answer would be that it is defined as a course that does not use a single synthetic pesticide, fertilizer, herbicide, or other artificial chemical treatment. In this definition, however, it should also be known that organic golf is only one aspect of organic care. This is done through as the interviewee says 

“From recycling to,  we try to go as much electric as we can, try to stay away from petroleum products. There’s a lot of little things that we do. Say downed trees for instance. We put vegetable oil in the chainsaws to cut up the branches and whatnot.  We try to take all the branches that have come down and we chip them and recycle them back into the golf course and pathways and we try and, it’s almost like a cycle, trying to keep everything healthy and hand it off to another generation where it’s  [in a]  a  great place and healthy”

Rather than bore you with the semantics of how organic golf is procured we can move on to the challenges this approach faces and why the popularity hasn’t arrived for this way of thinking. Part of it is that the level and type of success with organic management is context-dependent, the development of organic management techniques requires time and investment, and even though CGCS says it ‘works’ golfers would have to adapt to a new aesthetic and there would be playability adaptations. The biggest problem is that going organic is optional, and requires investments that aren’t especially safe but young superintendents still argue for their case. While organic golf still tries to find its voice, and the time, money, and Augusta National syndrome persist it seems the barriers are too high, but for an informed golfer it’s good to know that organic courses are viable and present.

Calling our Shot

The future of environmentally friendly golf is in our hands, and it promises a harmonious coexistence of the sport we love and the planet we cherish. Here are a few ways you can contribute to this promising future. Try to stay informed about technological advancements in golf that promote sustainability. Encourage your course to adopt tools like drones and AI to optimize resource management and reduce environmental impact. Support golf equipment manufacturers that are researching and developing sustainable materials for clubs, balls, and course furnishings. Look for eco-friendly options when upgrading your gear. Advocate for golf courses to become biodiversity hotspots. Supporting these initiatives can lead to not only more scenic courses but also conservation efforts in your region. Back golf courses that set ambitious goals for carbon neutrality. Contribute by reducing your own carbon footprint through practices like carpooling to the course, using electric golf carts, and minimizing waste. Finally, be a vocal advocate for environmentally friendly golf. Share your passion for sustainable golfing on social media, participate in local eco-friendly events, and encourage your fellow golfers to do the same.
